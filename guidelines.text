Thesis Structure

Cover page Should not have a page number. If you start numbering from the cover page, only the first 60 pages will be evaluated. Nothing beyond that.

Starting from abstract to list of abbreviations must use a different numbering system (ex: roman numerals).

Introduction to conclusion will be the main content that has 60 pages (these will use the regular indo-arabic numbering).

Follow TTT. Tell what you are going to tell; Tell it; Tell what you told. Do not put any table/figure that is not done by you or add unnecessary definitions/theory as this can simply be cited.

All diagrams must have a small description describing what it illustrates.

Do not put important things in the appendix. Examiners usually do not even look at the appendix. Only additional things should be placed in it.


Divide your entire thesis into three parts
Part 1 – Coversheet to List of Glossaries (For numbering use lowercase roman letters)
Coversheet no number
Numbering starts from the abstract to list of glossaries
Part 2 – Introduction to Conclusion (60 pages maximum)
Part 3 – Appendices (Numbered using uppercase roman letters)
References (not bibliography) – References are the ones which correspond to the citations in the document, referencing style is Harvard reference, not included in the 60 page count.
Appendix 1 onwards (no limit for page count when comes to appendix)
Note – Above is just an indicative structure of your thesis. The topics and sub topics might change depending on the type of project that you do. At the end you need to cover everything that is necessary for the marking scheme. How you cover and what you cover is up to you as far as there is a logical flow.

PART II – Main Body
Numbered with Arabic numerals (1, 2, 3…). All headings use Times New Roman, with the formatting hierarchy specified below.

CHAPTER 1 – INTRODUCTION
📏 Length: ~8 pages total
📚 Citation Guidance: Harvard style; minimum 1 citation per factual sentence; use recent (≤ 5 years) authoritative sources (e.g., ACM, IEEE, Springer, Elsevier).
🎯 Goal: Introduce the research domain, define the problem, explain motivation and gaps, outline contributions, challenges, research questions, aim, and objectives.

1.1 Chapter Overview
Briefly introduce what this chapter contains. Summarise the flow in 3–4 sentences (e.g., background → problem → motivation → gap → contributions → aim & objectives).
Explain why introducing the research clearly sets the stage for the rest of the dissertation. Follow the TTT principle (Tell what you’re going to tell; Tell it; Tell what you told).

1.2 Problem Background (~1½ pages total)
Introduce the broader research domain, break it into logical subtopics, and support each paragraph with recent, credible citations. Use clear sub-sectioning for readability.
1.2.1 Context of the Research Domain
Describe the overall context in which your research is situated.
Introduce the broader field, its significance, and recent developments.
Define any key concepts using authoritative sources.
Keep this section ~½ page.
Example (paraphrased):
“In recent years, explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) has gained significant attention within medical imaging, driven by the need to improve transparency and trust in automated diagnostic systems (Author et al., 2023; Author & Author, 2022).”

1.2.2 Subdomain / Key Technological Context
Zoom into the specific subdomain or technology relevant to your project (e.g., federated learning, multimodal fusion, anomaly detection).
Define technical concepts precisely.
Reference foundational and recent works.
Position where your topic fits in the subdomain. (~½ page)

1.2.3 Practical / Real-World Context
Describe industry, regulatory, or societal context, including:
Real-world applications
Gaps in deployment or adoption
Emerging trends
Use policy papers, standards (e.g., BCS, GDPR, WHO), or industry reports for credibility. (~½ page)
📌 Tip: Keep paragraphs tight but well-cited. Total for 1.2 ≈ 1½ pages.

1.3 Problem Definition (½ – ⅔ page)
Present a clear, precise definition of the problem your research addresses:
Why this is a problem in the chosen domain
Technical/methodological aspects involved
Scope and boundaries (what is included/excluded)
Support with citations showing the problem is known but unresolved.

1.3.1 Problem Statement
Write one concise, strong sentence summarising the core problem.
Example:
“There is currently no effective method for integrating multimodal XAI outputs to support radiologists in diagnosing complex brain tumours in real time.”
This should read like a gap in a single line, setting up the motivation.

1.4 Research Motivation
Explain why this problem matters — technically, socially, or personally.
Technical motivation: e.g., current approaches fail to address XYZ.
Societal motivation: e.g., impact on healthcare outcomes.
Personal motivation: professional experience relevant to the problem.
Maintain a professional, academic tone even when including personal motivation.

1.5 Research Gap
Clearly identify and justify gaps in existing work.
Use bullet points or short subheadings for each gap. For each gap:
State the gap in bold.
Justify why it is a gap with literature and logical reasoning.
Use separate paragraphs for different justifications.
Example structure:
Lack of Multimodal Fusion in XAI Models
Existing studies mainly focus on unimodal interpretability, neglecting cross-modal fusion, which is essential for complex clinical reasoning (Author et al., 2023; Author, 2021).
Limited Evaluation Protocols for Real-Time Settings
Current benchmarks do not assess interpretability under streaming constraints (Author & Author, 2022).
👉 Keep this structured, scannable, and heavily cited.

1.6 Contribution to the Body of Knowledge
State how your work advances research knowledge. Divide into two clear sub-sections.
1.6.1 Contribution to the Research Domain
Explain how your work contributes methodologically, theoretically, or empirically (e.g., new model, evaluation framework, dataset). Cite related work to highlight novelty.
1.6.2 Contribution to the Problem Domain
Describe how your work provides practical solutions or evidence addressing the specific problem in your context (e.g., supporting radiologists, improving security workflows). Provide evidence of real-world significance.
📌 Tip: Use further subheadings if multiple contributions exist under each category.

1.7 Research Challenges
Identify the main challenges you expect to address.
For each challenge:
Bold the challenge as a subheading.
Provide explanation and justification in a separate paragraph.
Example:
Challenge 1: Heterogeneous Data Modalities
The data sources vary widely in format and resolution, making joint representation difficult (Author et al., 2023).
Challenge 2: Real-Time Interpretability Constraints
Existing methods are not optimised for live inference scenarios (Author, 2022).

1.8 Research Questions
List precise, answerable research questions aligned with your problem and gaps. Number them clearly.
Example:
RQ1: How can multimodal fusion techniques be adapted to generate unified XAI outputs for radiology images?
RQ2: What evaluation metrics can effectively assess interpretability of fused outputs in real time?
These form the backbone of the dissertation.

1.9 Research Aim
State the overall aim in one sentence, using Design–Develop–Evaluate structure.
Example:
“The aim of this research is to design, develop, and evaluate a multimodal explainable AI framework to support real-time brain tumour diagnosis in MRI and CT imaging.”
Follow this with a short paragraph elaborating the aim in context.

1.10 Research Objectives
List SMART objectives (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound).
Start from problem identification, not literature.
Example:
RO1: Identify the limitations of existing unimodal XAI methods in radiology interpretation.
RO2: Design a multimodal fusion pipeline combining MRI and CT explanations.
RO3: Develop and implement the proposed pipeline using [technologies].
RO4: Evaluate the framework using standard interpretability metrics and real-time benchmarks.

1.11 Chapter Summary
Summarise key points in 1 short paragraph:
Context and problem
Motivation and gap
Contributions and challenges
Aim, questions, and objectives
Provide a transition sentence to Chapter 2 (Literature Review).

📌 Additional Notes for Chapter 1:
Background: 1½ pages, split into subsections.

Rest: concise, structured with bullets and subheadings.
Heavy citation density.
Times New Roman, 12 pt, 1.5 spacing, justified alignment.
